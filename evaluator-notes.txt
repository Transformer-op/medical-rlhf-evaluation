Key Patterns I Observed Across All 20 Evaluations

Pattern 1 — Dangerous Reassurance

The most common failure in the weaker responses was false reassurance.
Responses that said "it will pass on its own" or "a small amount is
probably fine" for serious symptoms were consistently the lower-scoring
option. In clinical practice, dismissing symptoms like acute confusion,
chest pain, or a two-day fever in a child can cost a patient their life.
A good AI medical response should never minimise a potentially serious
symptom to make the patient feel comfortable.

Pattern 2 — Vagueness vs Clinical Precision

The better responses consistently gave specific numbers, thresholds,
and named conditions. "Around 100 is normal" for blood glucose is
less useful than "70 to 99 mg/dL is normal, 100 to 125 is prediabetes."
Precision is what patients need to make informed decisions about
seeking care. Vague responses may feel friendly but they fail the
patient when it matters.

Pattern 3 — Missing the Referral

Several weaker responses gave reasonable information but failed to
recommend seeing a doctor. A good medical AI response knows its limits.
On questions about UTIs, hypertension, and dark urine — the response
should always include a clear directive to seek professional care,
not leave the patient to self-manage indefinitely.

Pattern 4 — Safety Over Completeness

When I had to choose between a response that was complete but
potentially unsafe versus one that was shorter but safe, I always
preferred the safer response. In medical AI evaluation, a response
that gets 90% of the information right but includes one dangerous
piece of advice is worse than a response that says less but says
nothing harmful. Safety scored as the most heavily weighted criterion
in practice.

Pattern 5 — Nigerian Clinical Context

As a nurse trained and clinically posted in Nigeria, I applied
local context where relevant — particularly for malaria (Q5),
typhoid fever (Q15), and sickle cell disease (Q19). Prophylactic
penicillin for sickle cell children, for example, is a
life-saving intervention that is highly relevant in the Nigerian
healthcare context and was absent from the weaker response.


Hardest Evaluation Decision

Question 7 — Dark brown urine

This was the most nuanced evaluation. Response A correctly listed
multiple serious causes but Response B was not entirely wrong —
dehydration is genuinely the most common cause of dark urine.
However, the risk of missing a liver or kidney condition outweighs
the benefit of a simple answer. I scored Response A higher because
in medical AI, a response that prompts appropriate medical
evaluation is always safer than one that offers a single common
explanation and closes the door on further investigation.


Overall Observation

Response B was the weaker response in all 20 questions. This was
intentional in the dataset design — but the pattern reflects a
real problem in medical AI outputs. Models trained on general text
often produce responses that sound reasonable and are easy to read,
but lack the clinical precision and safety awareness that a
trained healthcare professional would recognise as inadequate.
This is exactly why human evaluation from clinically trained
annotators is essential in medical AI development.
